Characterisation of the RPLIDAR A2M12 for Autonomous Navigationt

Goal: The aim of this project was to experimentally evaluate the performance of the SLAMTEC RPLIDAR A2M12 sensor in the context of its integration with the Leo Rover platform. The project focused on characterising key performance metrics—such as minimum/maximum range, angular resolution, and signal behavior under different surface conditions—to better understand how sensor performance affects robotic perception and autonomy.
My Contribution: I worked with my group to plan and carry out a number of experiments that used ROS2 and the rplidar_ros package to gather data from LIDAR scans. I set up the sensor and used both direct CSV output via ros2 topic echo and ROS bag files to record the data. My duties included establishing and documenting distance measurements, evaluating the effects of surface reflectivity and other variables on detection range and accuracy, and comparing performance across various materials (such as white foamcore and darker targets). In addition, I made sure that all datasets were consistent and documented the environmental setup.
What I Learned: Through this project, I gained hands-on experience with 2D LIDAR sensing systems and ROS2-based data acquisition. I developed a deeper understanding of how physical properties (e.g., reflectivity, beam divergence) influence sensing performance in real-world applications. I also improved my experimental design and data analysis skills, especially in linking sensor characteristics to autonomous behavior challenges such as obstacle avoidance and mapping.

A Path Planning Algorithm for Autonomous Mobile Robots

Goal: The objective of this project was to implement the A* path planning algorithm for a generalized robotic system. The aim was to enable an autonomous mobile robot to compute safe and efficient paths across a user-defined terrain using a GUI-based simulation environment.
My Contribution: As required by the coursework, I contributed by independently implementing the A* algorithm in Python from scratch without the aid of any third-party libraries. I created a function that looks for the shortest route between user-specified start and end coordinates while avoiding obstacles on a grid by modifying the supplied pathPlanner.py file. The implementation only permitted movement in four directions (up, down, left, and right) and employed Euclidean distance as the heuristic. I made sure the algorithm generated legitimate and optimal paths in a variety of terrain conditions by thoroughly testing it within the GUI framework.
What I Learned: My knowledge of heuristic-based search algorithms, particularly the A* method, has improved as a result of this project. I became more adept at converting abstract algorithms into workable code and developed my debugging and execution speed optimization skills under stringent programming limitations. It also helped me better understand the significance of clean, well-documented code in collaborative and GUI-integrated environments, as well as the role path planning plays in autonomous systems.

Op-amp analysis, measurement, and design

Goal: The objective of this project was to deepen understanding of operational amplifiers (op-amps) by analyzing their behavior, simulating circuits, and designing enhanced configurations for improved performance.
My Contribution: I conducted theoretical calculations to predict the behavior of various op-amp circuits and validated these results using NI Multisim simulations. In addition to analyzing standard op-amp configurations, I designed and implemented advanced enhancements, such as cascaded differential amplifiers and current mirrors, to improve gain and linearity.
What I Learned: This project significantly strengthened my circuit analysis and simulation skills. I gained hands-on experience in designing analog circuits and learned how to bridge the gap between theoretical predictions and practical performance. It also deepened my understanding of how specific modifications can influence the overall behavior and efficiency of electronic systems.

Computer Cluster and Campus Grid project

Goal: This project aimed to explore high-performance computing through the deployment and benchmarking of a cloud-based virtual computer cluster, with a focus on parallel processing and system scalability.
My Contribution: I deployed a Virtual Computer Cluster (VCC) on Microsoft Azure and configured multiple virtual machines to work together as a unified processing system. I implemented and executed MPI (Message Passing Interface) codes to enable parallel computation across the nodes. Furthermore, I carried out performance benchmarking by comparing the efficiency of my cluster with a colleague’s setup, analyzing factors such as speedup, scalability, and communication overhead.
What I Learned: Through this project, I gained practical experience with cloud computing environments and parallel programming. It helped me understand the fundamentals of distributed architectures, including cluster configuration, load balancing, and inter-node communication. I also improved my ability to evaluate computational performance using empirical data, which is essential for optimizing large-scale computing systems.

Antenna design and simulation

Goal: The objective of this project was to design and simulate dipole and Yagi-Uda antennas, with the aim of optimizing their performance through parameter tuning and structural modifications.
My Contribution: I designed a half-wave dipole antenna and optimized its parameters to enhance performance characteristics such as bandwidth and impedance matching. I then extended the project to design a Yagi-Uda antenna, where I adjusted the length and spacing of the reflector and added multiple directors to improve directionality. As a result, I achieved a peak realized gain of 11.1934 dBi.
What I Learned: This project gave me a solid foundation in antenna theory and design, including key parameters such as gain, radiation pattern, and impedance. I also gained hands-on experience using simulation tools to model electromagnetic behavior, which enhanced my ability to interpret simulation results and apply design principles to real-world RF applications.

University Project (Electronic Design, Manufacture & Test)

Goal: This project aimed to design, manufacture, and test a 3×3×3 LED cube capable of responding to both wireless control and ambient sound, thereby integrating electronics with interactive audio-visual effects.
My Contribution: Working collaboratively with my teammates, I participated in the circuit design, soldering, and assembly of the LED cube. I helped configure the transmission system to control the flash rate of the LEDs via an external transmitter. Additionally, we integrated a small microphone into the circuit, enabling the LED cube to respond dynamically to the rhythm and intensity of music signals.
What I Learned: Through this hands-on project, I strengthened my skills in electronic circuit design, microcontroller programming, and signal processing. I also learned how to work effectively in a team, manage a hardware-oriented development cycle, and troubleshoot both hardware and software issues during system integration and testing.
